1) Cells and Tissues: Structure and function foundation

A) Key Points (Must Memorise)

  • Organisation: cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism.
  • Eukaryotic cell structures: nucleus, cytoplasm, cell membrane, mitochondria, ribosomes.
  • Specialised cells have adaptations that match function (structure–function link).
  • Surface area : volume ratio affects diffusion rate (small cells exchange faster).
  • DNA is genetic material; genes are sections of DNA that code for proteins.
  • Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells for growth and repair.
  • Meiosis produces four haploid gametes and introduces genetic variation.
  • Protein synthesis occurs by transcription and translation to build proteins from amino acids.

B) Definitions (exam wording)

  • Cell: smallest structural and functional unit of an organism.
  • Tissue: group of similar cells working together to carry out a function.
  • Organ: structure made of different tissues working together.
  • Specialised cell: cell adapted to carry out a specific function.
  • DNA: molecule that carries genetic information.
  • Gene: section of DNA that codes for a protein (or characteristic).
  • Chromosome: long coiled DNA molecule carrying many genes.
  • Allele: different form of a gene.
  • Mitosis: nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter cells.
  • Meiosis: nuclear division producing four haploid gametes with genetic variation.
  • Diploid: cell with two sets of chromosomes.
  • Haploid: cell with one set of chromosomes.
  • mRNA: messenger RNA that carries the genetic code from DNA to ribosomes.
  • Transcription: copying a gene’s DNA base sequence to form mRNA.
  • Translation: assembling amino acids into a protein using mRNA at ribosomes.
  • Codon: sequence of three bases on mRNA that codes for one amino acid.
  • Mutation: random change in DNA that may create a new allele.

C) Core Understanding

Cell structures (function statements)

  • Nucleus contains genetic material (DNA) and controls cell activities.
  • Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration (release energy).
  • Ribosomes are the site of protein synthesis.
  • Cell membrane controls movement of substances into and out of the cell.

DNA, genes and chromosomes

  • DNA is the genetic material found in the nucleus (in chromosomes).
  • A chromosome is a long DNA molecule coiled up; it contains many genes.
  • A gene is a section of DNA; the base sequence provides a code for making proteins.
  • Proteins (including enzymes) control cell reactions and determine cell structure and function.

Protein synthesis (exam sequence)

  1. Transcription occurs in the nucleus: the base sequence of a gene is copied to make mRNA.
  2. mRNA leaves the nucleus and attaches to a ribosome in the cytoplasm.
  3. Translation: the ribosome reads mRNA in codons (triplets of bases).
  4. Each codon codes for a specific amino acid.
  5. Amino acids join in the correct order to form a polypeptide which folds into a protein.

Mitosis (PMAT) — growth and repair

  • Before mitosis, DNA replication occurs so each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids.
  • Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells (same genes/alleles as parent cell).
PMAT (key events):
  • Prophase: chromosomes condense; nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle forms.
  • Metaphase: chromosomes line up at the equator; spindle attaches to centromeres.
  • Anaphase: sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
  • Telophase: nuclei reform; chromosomes decondense.
  • Cytokinesis: cytoplasm divides → two identical cells.

Meiosis — gametes and variation

  • Meiosis produces four haploid gametes that are genetically different.
  • It halves chromosome number: diploid → haploid.
  • Meiosis I separates homologous chromosome pairs (chromosome number halved).
  • Meiosis II separates sister chromatids (similar to mitosis).
  • Fertilisation restores diploid number when gamete nuclei fuse.

Mutation and variation (link marks)

  • A mutation changes DNA base sequence → may change codons → may change amino acid sequence → may change protein shape/function → may change phenotype.
  • Variation can be genetic (alleles inherited) and environmental (diet, exercise).

D) Organ Structures & Functions (where relevant)

Common specialised cells (adaptations → function)

  • Red blood cell: biconcave shape (large SA), no nucleus (more haemoglobin), flexible → transports oxygen efficiently.
  • Neurone: long axon, dendrites, myelin sheath → rapid transmission of impulses.
  • Ciliated epithelial cell: cilia beat to move mucus → clears airways.
  • Sperm cell: tail, many mitochondria, acrosome → fertilisation.

E) Practical / Skills

  • Microscope: calculate magnification: magnification = image size ÷ real size.
  • Drawing cells: clear lines, labels, no shading; include scale where required.

F) Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • ❌ “Mitochondria make energy” → ✔ “mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration which releases energy”.
  • ❌ “Osmosis is diffusion of salt” → ✔ “osmosis is diffusion of water through a partially permeable membrane”.
  • Confusing mitosis (two identical cells) with meiosis (four haploid gametes + variation).
  • For protein synthesis, naming only “ribosomes” loses marks — you must state transcription and translation in order.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2 marks: Define tissue / organ; define gene; define mitosis.
  • 3 marks: Explain why cells are small (link SA:V to diffusion).
  • 4 marks: Describe stages of mitosis (PMAT) or compare mitosis vs meiosis.
  • 6 marks: Explain how mitosis produces identical cells / explain protein synthesis / explain how a mutation can change phenotype.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how red blood cells are adapted for oxygen transport. (6)

Full-marks answer:
  1. Red blood cells have a biconcave shape which increases surface area for diffusion of oxygen.
  2. They contain haemoglobin which binds oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin.
  3. They have no nucleus, giving more space for haemoglobin.
  4. Their membrane is flexible so they can squeeze through capillaries.
  5. This maintains a short diffusion pathway and allows rapid delivery of oxygen.
  6. Therefore they transport oxygen efficiently from lungs to tissues.
Mark-by-mark: biconcave/SA (1) + diffusion link (1) + haemoglobin (1) + oxyhaemoglobin (1) + no nucleus/space (1) + flexible/capillaries or conclusion (1).
Weak answer: “Red blood cells carry oxygen because they have haemoglobin and are round.”
Why it loses marks: missing adaptations + no links to diffusion/SA:V/capillaries.
Upgrade: add biconcave shape + no nucleus + flexibility + link each to faster transport.

2) Biological Molecules: Understanding chemical components biochemistry

A) Key Points

  • Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, water, vitamins, minerals are required for health.
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts with specific active sites.
  • Enzyme activity depends on temperature and pH; extreme conditions denature enzymes.

B) Definitions

  • Enzyme: protein that speeds up a reaction without being used up.
  • Active site: region of enzyme where substrate binds.
  • Denature: enzyme changes shape so active site no longer fits substrate.

C) Core Understanding

  • Carbohydrates (e.g., starch) → energy; broken down to glucose.
  • Lipids (fats/oils) → energy store, insulation, cell membranes.
  • Proteins → growth and repair; enzymes; made of amino acids.

D) Structures & Functions (where relevant)

Enzyme factors (what to write)

  • Higher temperature increases kinetic energy → more collisions → rate increases (up to optimum).
  • Above optimum: bonds break → enzyme denatures → active site changes shape → rate decreases.
  • pH change alters ionic bonds → active site changes shape → rate decreases.

E) Practical / Skills

  • Investigate enzyme rate (e.g., amylase) by changing temperature or pH.
  • Control variables: enzyme concentration, substrate concentration, volume, mixing, time intervals.
  • Graph: rate vs temperature (optimum curve); identify optimum and denaturation region.

F) Common Mistakes

  • ❌ “Enzymes die” → ✔ “enzymes denature; active site changes shape”.
  • ❌ “pH speeds up all enzymes” → ✔ “each enzyme has an optimum pH”.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: Define enzyme/denature; state one function of proteins.
  • 3: Describe effect of temperature on enzyme activity.
  • 4: Explain why rate falls after optimum temperature.
  • 6: Evaluate an enzyme experiment and suggest improvements (controls, repeats, accuracy).

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how temperature affects enzyme activity. (6)

  1. As temperature increases, molecules gain kinetic energy.
  2. This increases the frequency of successful collisions between enzyme and substrate.
  3. So the reaction rate increases up to an optimum temperature.
  4. Above the optimum, heat breaks bonds in the enzyme.
  5. The enzyme denatures so the active site changes shape.
  6. Substrate no longer fits → fewer enzyme-substrate complexes → rate decreases.
Weak answer: “Hotter = faster until it stops working.”
Upgrade: add kinetic energy, collisions, optimum, denature, active site shape.

3) Movement of Substances: In and out of cells transport

A) Key Points

  • Diffusion, osmosis, and active transport are key cell processes.
  • Rate depends on concentration gradient, surface area, temperature, diffusion distance.
  • Active transport uses energy (ATP) to move against a concentration gradient.

B) Definitions

  • Diffusion: net movement of particles from higher to lower concentration.
  • Osmosis: diffusion of water through a partially permeable membrane from dilute to concentrated solution.
  • Active transport: movement from low to high concentration using energy from respiration.

C) Core Understanding

  • Osmosis causes cells to gain/lose water depending on water potential (relative concentration).
  • Active transport is used in intestine and kidney tubules for reabsorption/uptake.

D) Organ Structures & Functions

Villi (absorption) – structure-function keywords

  • Many villi + microvilli → large surface area.
  • Epithelium one cell thick → short diffusion distance.
  • Good blood supply → maintains concentration gradient.
  • Lacteal → absorbs fatty acids and glycerol (lipids).

E) Practical / Skills

  • Osmosis practical (potato cylinders): measure mass change vs sucrose concentration.
  • Independent: concentration; dependent: % mass change; controls: time, temperature, size, blotting.
  • Graph: % change vs concentration; identify isotonic point (~0% change).

F) Common Mistakes

  • ❌ “Particles move to equalise” (vague) → ✔ “net movement down a concentration gradient”.
  • ❌ “Active transport is diffusion” → ✔ “requires energy and carrier proteins”.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: Define osmosis; state one factor affecting diffusion.
  • 3: Explain why villi increase absorption.
  • 4: Compare diffusion and active transport (energy + direction of gradient).
  • 6: Interpret an osmosis graph and explain results using correct terms.

H) Exam Technique

Worked 6-mark example

Question: A potato cylinder loses mass in a concentrated sucrose solution. Explain. (6)

  1. The potato cells contain a more dilute solution than the sucrose solution outside.
  2. Water moves by osmosis through the partially permeable cell membrane.
  3. Water moves from higher to lower water potential (from dilute to concentrated).
  4. So water leaves the potato cells.
  5. The cells become flaccid (lose turgor) and the cylinder loses mass.
  6. Therefore the net movement of water is out of the potato into the sucrose solution.

4) Nutrition and Energy: Diet and metabolism digestion

A) Key Points

  • Balanced diet supplies: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, vitamins, minerals, fibre, water.
  • Digestion: large insoluble molecules → small soluble molecules.
  • Enzymes are specific and work best at optimum pH/optimum temperature.
  • Absorption occurs mainly in the small intestine via villi.
  • Energy balance: intake vs expenditure affects body mass.

B) Definitions

  • Balanced diet: provides all nutrients in correct proportions for health.
  • Digestion: breakdown of large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules.
  • Absorption: movement of digested molecules through intestine wall into blood/lymph.
  • Assimilation: use of absorbed molecules by cells (e.g. amino acids → proteins).
  • Metabolism: all chemical reactions in the body (includes respiration, synthesis, breakdown).

C) Core Understanding

  • Carbohydrates: energy source (respiration); stored as glycogen in liver/muscle.
  • Lipids: long-term energy store; insulation; cell membranes.
  • Proteins: growth/repair; enzymes/hormones; excess amino acids cannot be stored → deamination → urea.
  • Fibre: adds bulk; aids peristalsis; reduces constipation risk.
  • Water: solvent; transport medium; temperature regulation.

D) Organ Structures & Functions

Digestive system organs (part → function keywords)

  • Mouth: chewing (mechanical digestion); saliva contains amylase.
  • Oesophagus: peristalsis moves bolus to stomach.
  • Stomach: churning; protease; hydrochloric acid (low pH optimum for protease; kills bacteria).
  • Liver: produces bile (emulsifies fats; neutralises acid); stores glycogen; deaminates amino acids.
  • Gall bladder: stores/concentrates bile.
  • Pancreas: produces digestive enzymes; secretes bicarbonate to neutralise acid (where specified).
  • Small intestine: completes digestion; absorption via villi.
  • Large intestine: absorbs water; forms faeces.

Enzymes (substrate → products)

EnzymeSubstrateProductsWhere works (typical)
AmylaseStarchMaltose (then glucose)Mouth / small intestine
ProteaseProteinAmino acidsStomach / small intestine
LipaseLipidsFatty acids + glycerolSmall intestine
Bile is NOT an enzyme. It emulsifies fats (bigger droplets → smaller droplets) to increase surface area for lipase, and it neutralises stomach acid.

E) Practical / Skills

  • Food tests (starch/ sugars/ protein/ lipid) + interpreting unknowns.
  • Plan digestion experiments (e.g., amylase + starch): control temperature, pH, volumes; repeat and average.
  • Data: interpret digestion rate graphs; calculate % change; evaluate diet/energy labels.

F) Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • Wrong: “bile digests fat” → Write: bile emulsifies fats; lipase digests fats.
  • Wrong: “absorption happens in stomach” → Write: absorption mainly in small intestine (villi).
  • Wrong: “assimilation = absorption” → Write: absorption into blood; assimilation is use by cells.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: Name organ producing bile; define assimilation.
  • 3: Describe peristalsis.
  • 4: Explain how bile helps fat digestion.
  • 6: Explain villi adaptations for absorption (SA, thin wall, blood supply, lacteal).
  • Data: interpret enzyme graphs / label digestion organs / food label calculations.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how the small intestine is adapted for absorption of digested food. (6)

  1. The small intestine has many villi and microvilli, giving a large surface area.
  2. The epithelium is one cell thick, giving a short diffusion distance.
  3. Each villus has a rich capillary network that carries away glucose and amino acids.
  4. This maintains a concentration gradient for diffusion into the blood.
  5. Villi contain lacteals to absorb fatty acids and glycerol into the lymph.
  6. Therefore these adaptations increase the rate of absorption.
Weak answer: “The intestine is long so it absorbs more.”
Upgrade: use the villi adaptation list and link each feature to diffusion/transport.

5) Respiration: Energy release in cells cell energy

A) Key Points

  • Respiration is a chemical process that releases energy in cells.
  • Aerobic respiration uses oxygen and releases more energy than anaerobic respiration.
  • Anaerobic respiration in muscles produces lactic acid; causes fatigue; creates oxygen debt.

B) Definitions

  • Respiration: chemical reactions in cells that break down glucose to release energy.
  • Aerobic respiration: respiration using oxygen.
  • Anaerobic respiration: respiration without oxygen.
  • Oxygen debt: extra oxygen needed after exercise to oxidise lactic acid.

C) Core Understanding

Word equations (must know):
Aerobic: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy)
Anaerobic (muscle): glucose → lactic acid (+ energy)
  • Energy is used for: muscle contraction, active transport, synthesis reactions, maintaining body temperature.
  • During intense exercise oxygen supply may be insufficient → anaerobic respiration increases → lactic acid builds up.
  • Oxygen debt is repaid by increased breathing and heart rate after exercise.

D) Structures & Functions

  • Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration; more mitochondria in active tissues (e.g., muscle).

E) Practical / Skills

  • Use pulse/breathing rate data to infer respiration demand during exercise.
  • Graph interpretation: recovery curves; compare rest vs exercise.

F) Common Mistakes

  • Breathing ≠ respiration (breathing is ventilation; respiration is chemical reactions in cells).
  • Missing “energy released” when asked for respiration equation.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: State products of aerobic respiration; define oxygen debt.
  • 3: Explain why breathing rate increases during exercise.
  • 4: Compare aerobic and anaerobic respiration.
  • 6: Explain oxygen debt and recovery after vigorous exercise.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain what is meant by oxygen debt and how it is repaid. (6)

  1. During vigorous exercise, muscles may not get enough oxygen for aerobic respiration.
  2. Some energy is released by anaerobic respiration.
  3. Anaerobic respiration produces lactic acid in muscles.
  4. Oxygen debt is the extra oxygen needed after exercise to oxidise lactic acid.
  5. Breathing and heart rate remain high to deliver more oxygen and remove carbon dioxide.
  6. As lactic acid is broken down, oxygen debt is repaid and rates return to normal.
Weak answer: “Oxygen debt is when you need more oxygen after running.”
Upgrade: add anaerobic respiration → lactic acid → oxidised using extra oxygen.

6) Gas Exchange: Breathing mechanisms lungs

A) Key Points

  • Gas exchange occurs in the alveoli by diffusion.
  • Efficient exchange requires large surface area, short diffusion distance, steep gradients.
  • Ventilation changes thorax volume and pressure to move air in/out of lungs.

B) Definitions

  • Ventilation: movement of air into and out of lungs.
  • Alveolus: air sac where gas exchange occurs.
  • Diffusion: net movement from high to low concentration.

C) Core Understanding

  • Oxygen diffuses from alveoli (higher concentration) to blood (lower concentration).
  • Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood (higher concentration) to alveoli (lower concentration).
  • Ventilation and blood flow maintain steep concentration gradients.

D) Organ Structures & Functions (Lungs)

Lung structure (label + function):
  • Trachea: cartilage rings keep airway open; cilia + mucus trap and remove dust/pathogens.
  • Bronchi: carry air into each lung.
  • Bronchioles: smaller tubes leading to alveoli.
  • Alveoli: gas exchange surface.
  • Capillaries: bring deoxygenated blood; carry away oxygenated blood.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange. (6)

  1. There are many alveoli, giving a large surface area for diffusion.
  2. Alveolar and capillary walls are one cell thick, giving a short diffusion distance.
  3. The surface is moist so gases dissolve before diffusing.
  4. A dense capillary network provides a good blood supply.
  5. Blood flow removes oxygen and delivers carbon dioxide, maintaining a steep concentration gradient.
  6. Ventilation replaces alveolar air, also maintaining the gradient, so diffusion is rapid.
Weak answer: “Alveoli are small so gases move fast.”
Upgrade: list adaptations with diffusion links (SA, thin wall, blood supply, ventilation).

7) Internal Transport: Circulatory system heart + blood

A) Key Points

  • Humans have double circulation: pulmonary + systemic.
  • Heart is a pump; valves prevent backflow.
  • Blood transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones; removes CO₂ and urea; distributes heat.

B) Definitions

  • Double circulation: blood passes through the heart twice per complete circuit.
  • Artery: carries blood away from the heart (high pressure).
  • Vein: carries blood toward the heart; has valves.
  • Capillary: exchange vessel with one-cell-thick walls.

C) Core Understanding

  • Pulmonary circulation: right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs → pulmonary vein → left atrium.
  • Systemic circulation: left ventricle → aorta → body → vena cava → right atrium.
  • Capillaries allow exchange by diffusion with body cells.

D) Organ Structures & Functions (Heart + Blood)

Heart chambers + major vessels:
  • Right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from vena cava.
  • Right ventricle pumps blood to lungs via pulmonary artery.
  • Left atrium receives oxygenated blood from pulmonary vein.
  • Left ventricle pumps blood to body via aorta (thick wall for high pressure).
  • Valves prevent backflow (one-way flow).
  • Coronary arteries supply heart muscle with oxygen and glucose.
VesselStructureFunction link
ArteryThick muscular wall; small lumenWithstands high pressure; maintains flow
VeinThin wall; large lumen; valvesReturns blood at low pressure; valves prevent backflow
CapillaryOne-cell-thick wall; narrow lumenShort diffusion distance; exchange with tissues
Blood components:
  • Plasma: transports dissolved substances (CO₂, urea, glucose, hormones, amino acids).
  • Red blood cells: haemoglobin transports oxygen.
  • White blood cells: defend against pathogens (phagocytosis/antibodies if required).
  • Platelets: clotting; prevents blood loss; stops entry of pathogens.

E) Practical / Skills

  • Measure pulse and recovery after exercise; calculate % increase; interpret graphs.
  • Evaluate risk factors for coronary heart disease (smoking, diet, exercise, obesity).

F) Common Mistakes

  • Pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood (common trap).
  • Not stating valves prevent backflow (must include for marks).

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: Name vessel leaving left ventricle; state function of platelets.
  • 3: Describe pathway of blood from vena cava to aorta.
  • 4: Explain why left ventricle wall is thicker than right ventricle wall.
  • 6: Explain how capillary structure makes exchange efficient.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain why capillaries are efficient exchange vessels. (6)

  1. Capillary walls are one cell thick, giving a short diffusion distance.
  2. The lumen is narrow so red blood cells pass close to the wall.
  3. They form extensive networks, giving a large surface area.
  4. Blood flow is slower in capillaries, allowing more time for diffusion.

10) Homeostasis and Excretion: Maintaining balance kidneys

A) Key Points

  • Homeostasis maintains a stable internal environment (water balance, blood glucose, temperature).
  • Excretion removes metabolic wastes: CO₂, urea, excess water and salts.
  • Kidney: ultrafiltration + selective reabsorption + water balance (ADH).

B) Definitions

  • Homeostasis: maintenance of a constant internal environment.
  • Excretion: removal of metabolic waste products from the body.
  • Ultrafiltration: pressure filtration at glomerulus into Bowman’s capsule.
  • Selective reabsorption: reabsorption of useful substances into blood.
  • ADH: hormone that increases water reabsorption in collecting ducts.
  • Negative feedback: response reverses a change to restore normal conditions.

C) Core Understanding

  • Excess amino acids are deaminated in liver → ammonia → converted to urea.
  • Urea is transported in blood to kidneys and excreted in urine.
  • Water balance controlled by ADH depending on hydration state.

D) Organ Structures & Functions (Kidneys)

Kidney gross structure:
  • Renal artery: brings blood to kidney.
  • Renal vein: carries filtered blood away.
  • Cortex: contains glomeruli, Bowman’s capsules, PCT/DCT.
  • Medulla: contains loops of Henle and collecting ducts.
  • Ureter: carries urine to bladder.
Nephron parts (label + function):
  • Glomerulus: high pressure → ultrafiltration of small molecules.
  • Bowman’s capsule: collects filtrate.
  • PCT: reabsorbs all glucose, most amino acids, ions; water follows.
  • Loop of Henle: establishes medulla gradient for water conservation.
  • DCT: further adjustment of ions/water.
  • Collecting duct: water reabsorption controlled by ADH; sets urine concentration.

Ultrafiltration + selective reabsorption (step chain)

  • High pressure in glomerulus forces water + small solutes into Bowman’s capsule.
  • Proteins and blood cells remain in blood (too large to pass filter).
  • Useful substances are reabsorbed into capillaries (often by active transport).
  • Amount of water reabsorbed varies based on ADH.

ADH control of water balance (negative feedback)

  • Dehydration → blood more concentrated → pituitary releases more ADH.
  • ADH increases collecting duct permeability → more water reabsorbed.
  • Urine becomes more concentrated; blood water content returns to normal → ADH decreases.

E) Practical / Skills

  • Interpret urine/blood data (glucose/protein/water) and link to kidney function.
  • Graph analysis: ADH levels vs urine concentration; describe trends using negative feedback.

F) Common Mistakes

  • Filtration is at glomerulus/Bowman’s capsule, not PCT.
  • Glucose is normally absent from urine because it is reabsorbed in PCT.

G) Exam Questions Bank

  • 1–2: Define excretion; name where ultrafiltration occurs.
  • 3: Describe what is filtered at the glomerulus.
  • 4: Explain why glucose is not present in urine in a healthy person.
  • 6: Explain ADH control of water balance using negative feedback.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how ADH controls water balance. (6)

  1. If blood water content is low, the person is dehydrated and blood is more concentrated.
  2. The pituitary gland releases more ADH.
  3. ADH increases the permeability of the collecting ducts.
  4. More water is reabsorbed from filtrate into the blood.
  5. A smaller volume of concentrated urine is produced.
  6. As blood water content returns to normal, less ADH is released (negative feedback).
Weak answer: “ADH makes you pee less.”
Upgrade: include pituitary + collecting ducts permeability + negative feedback chain.

H) How to Answer in the Exam (STRUCTURE)

Worked 6-mark example

Question: Explain how fertiliser run-off can lead to fish death in a lake. (6)

  1. Fertilisers add nitrates and phosphates to the lake, increasing nutrient levels.
  2. This causes rapid growth of algae (an algal bloom).
  3. The bloom blocks light, so underwater plants photosynthesise less and may die.
  4. Dead plants and algae are decomposed by microorganisms.
  5. Decomposers respire aerobically and use up dissolved oxygen in the water.
  6. Oxygen levels fall so fish cannot respire aerobically and many die (eutrophication).
Weak answer: “Fertiliser poisons fish.”
Upgrade: nutrient → algae bloom → decomposition → oxygen drop → fish die.
